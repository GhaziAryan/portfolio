Problem
Risk teams had their own workflows, their own terminology, their own tools. 300+ assessments a year, no shared language. Regulatory deadlines don't move. Every week a team spent re-inventing a process that already existed somewhere else was a week closer to a missed deadline nobody wanted to explain. It wasn't a tech problem. It was a people problem with regulatory consequences.
Approach
I time-blocked aggressively: half the week on discovery and stakeholder conversations, half on product execution. Built relationships with methodology partners first, then used that trust to refine requirements and drive adoption. Owned the BPMN model development for status and workflow, which turned out to be far more complex than initially scoped. Integrated learnings from the LAUNCH assessment to anticipate problems in the PLA build before they arrived.
Key tradeoff
We deferred custom reporting to Release 2 even though senior leadership wanted it upfront. Scoping was the critical module. It consolidated information across multiple platforms, served as the backbone of the workflow, and powered notifications, risks, diagnostics, and every downstream assessment page. Getting scoping right was non-negotiable, so that's where we focused. Reporting could wait. It did, and scoping shipped on time.

Side project / founder

Tape: a trading journal that reads your mind (well, your on-device LLM does)

I wanted to journal my trades, but every app felt like filling out a form. I wanted to just type, stream of consciousness, and have something smart extract the patterns. So I built it.

Journal
Positions
Insights
Hard part
Getting real value from a local LLM. The input field had to feel completely free-form while the model quietly structured everything behind the scenes. Lots of prompt fine-tuning and guardrail building to keep inference fast and output useful.
What failed
My first approach used a single long prompt that tried to do everything at once: tag the trade, detect sentiment, and generate advice. Latency was terrible and the output was generic. I broke it into a chain of focused prompts, each doing one thing well. Inference time dropped by 60%.

Point of view

The Agent Manager is coming.

AI tooling is changing what one person can ship. That's not hype, it's just what's happening. When you can scaffold a prototype in an afternoon, the boundary between "the person who defines the product" and "the person who builds it" starts to dissolve.

Product thinking, technical execution, and design intuition are merging into a single skill set. I call this role the Agent Manager. Not because you manage AI agents (though you might), but because you have the agency to go from problem to shipped product without waiting on a handoff chain.

I'm not saying every PM needs to learn Swift. I'm saying the PMs who can move fluidly between strategy and execution will have an edge. That's what I'm building toward.

Evidence: Tape

Built a full iOS app with on-device LLM inference. PM discovery, prompt engineering, Swift development, UX iteration. One person, idea to TestFlight.

Evidence: Grati

Photo-based gratitude app built in under two weeks. Defined the MVP, built with Swift + Supabase + Cursor, and iterated on UX until engagement jumped 40% in private testing.

Evidence: Daily practice

At Capital One, I use AI tooling for user research synthesis, acceptance criteria generation, and competitive analysis. Not as a shortcut. As a way to spend more time on the decisions that matter.
