Problem

Approach

Key tradeoff
We deferred custom reporting to Release 2 even though senior leadership wanted it upfront. Scoping was the critical module. It consolidated information across multiple platforms, served as the backbone of the workflow, and powered notifications, risks, diagnostics, and every downstream assessment page. Getting scoping right was non-negotiable, so that's where we focused. Reporting could wait. It did, and scoping shipped on time.

Side project / founder

Tape: an AI trading journal.

Every trading journal felt like filling out a form. I wanted to just type, stream of consciousness, and have something smart extract the patterns. So I built it.

Journal
Positions
Insights
Hard part
Getting accurate sentiment from a small on-device model. Handling failed parses gracefully. Chaining trades together even when entered out of order. Surfacing real behavioral insights that actually improve a trader's decisions, not generic "set a stop loss" advice. Presenting all of this while maintaining a clean, notebook-like feel.
Three engines
The insight engine went through three full rewrites:

v1 (stats-based): Serialized aggregated stats into the prompt. The model received numbers like "plan_quality: 3 days, avg P/L -$205." Problem: the tags were ambiguous. "plan_quality" fired on both "followed my plan" and "broke my rules," so the aggregated stats were misleading. The LLM echoed system jargon into user-facing text.

v2 (stats + human labels): Translated tag IDs to plain language and rewrote the system prompt to ban jargon. More readable, but still wrong, because the underlying data was still lossy from ambiguous tags.

v3 (raw notes): Feeds the LLM the trader's actual journal entries with outcomes. No stats aggregation. The model reads their real words and finds patterns itself. Added in-memory caching keyed by trade data hash. This is the current architecture.
v1 stats-based
40%

85%
Lesson
Don't aggregate data the model hasn't seen. Every layer of abstraction between the user's words and the LLM is a place where meaning gets lost. The best insights came when I stopped trying to pre-process and let the model read the raw text.

Point of view

The Agent Manager is coming.

AI tooling is changing what one person can ship. That's not hype, it's just what's happening. When you can scaffold a prototype in an afternoon, the boundary between "the person who defines the product" and "the person who builds it" starts to dissolve.

Product thinking, technical execution, and design intuition are merging into a single skill set. I call this role the Agent Manager. Not because you manage AI agents (though you might), but because you have the agency to go from problem to shipped product without waiting on a handoff chain.

I'm not saying every PM needs to learn Swift. I'm saying the PMs who can move fluidly between strategy and execution will have an edge. That's what I'm building toward.

Evidence: Tape

Full iOS app with on-device LLM inference. PM discovery, prompt engineering, Swift development, UX iteration. One person, idea to TestFlight.

Evidence: Grati

A photo-based gratitude app. On-device LLM generates live captions as you frame a shot, a delayed shutter (press and hold) fades the viewfinder to white. Idea to working prototype in a single day. The turning point where I realized vibe coding was real.

Evidence: Daily practice

At Capital One, I use AI tooling for user research synthesis, acceptance criteria generation, and competitive analysis. Not as a shortcut. As a way to spend more time on the decisions that matter.
